Real Morocco Prices vs Tourist Prices — The Complete 2025/2026 Breakdown
By Soufiane Elghazzouani — born in Demnate, raised in Agadir, living in Morocco

There are two price lists in Morocco.
The first one exists for tourists. It's the price written on no menu, quoted by every taxi driver who sees a foreign face, and charged at every restaurant facing a famous square. It's not printed anywhere. Everyone just knows it exists.
The second one is what locals actually pay. It's lower — sometimes by 20%, sometimes by 300%. The gap between them is not accidental. It's structural, consistent, and completely avoidable once you know what things actually cost.
This is that second list.


Why the Price Gap Exists
Morocco has a dual economy built around tourism. Locals and tourists move through the same cities, eat the same food, and use the same taxis — but they operate in parallel pricing systems that almost never intersect unless the tourist knows how to cross over.
Nobody will explain this to you at the airport. No official guide acknowledges it. Most travel blogs quote tourist prices as normal because their writers never knew the difference.
The gap exists because it works. A tourist who doesn't know that a petit taxi from the medina to their riad should cost 20 MAD will pay 80 MAD without question. A tourist who doesn't know that a bowl of harira costs 10 MAD at a local spot will pay 40 MAD at a café near Jemaa el-Fna and feel like they got a deal.
You are about to know the difference.

Food: What Things Actually Cost
Food is where the tourist premium is most visible and most avoidable. The same dish — genuinely the same dish, made the same way — costs dramatically different amounts depending on where you sit.
Tagine
The most iconic Moroccan dish and the most aggressively mispriced for tourists.
Local restaurant: 40–60 MAD Tourist restaurant: 80–150 MAD
The local version is often better. The tourist version costs more because of the view, the tablecloth, and the assumption that you don't know any better.
How to find local price tagine: walk away from any restaurant that has someone standing outside trying to invite you in. Walk away from any restaurant where the menu is in four languages and displayed in the window. Find the place with plastic chairs, no English anywhere, and every table full of Moroccans eating quickly. Order there.
Harira
The rich tomato and lentil soup that is Morocco's daily bread — eaten for breakfast, lunch, before iftar during Ramadan, and at every meal in between.
Local price: 10–20 MAD per bowl Tourist price: 25–40 MAD
A bowl of harira at a local spot costs less than a bottle of water at a tourist café. It is also one of the best things you will eat in Morocco. Find it at small spots without menus, order it with bread, eat it standing if necessary.
Bissara
Fava bean soup with olive oil and cumin, eaten primarily for breakfast in the colder months.
Local price: 10 MAD What tourists pay: 20–30 MAD when they find it at all
This one is almost impossible to find in tourist areas because it's not considered photogenic enough to market. Find it in local neighbourhood spots early in the morning. It costs almost nothing and it's extraordinary.
Sfenj
Moroccan doughnuts, fried fresh, eaten with tea in the morning.
Local price: 1–4 MAD per piece Tourist price: None — because tourists rarely find the real spots
Street vendors and small stalls sell these fresh every morning. The tourist version sold at cafés near main squares costs three to four times more and is usually not as fresh. Find a vendor with a crowd of locals around them and join the queue.
Street sandwich and snack spots
The backbone of local eating — grilled meat sandwiches, kefta, merguez, simple plates.
Local price: 10–20 MAD Tourist café equivalent: 40–60 MAD
These spots have no names, no signs in English, and no Instagram presence. They are identified by the smoke coming from the grill, the queue of people, and the complete absence of anyone trying to get your attention. Walk toward the smoke.

Coffee and Drinks
Coffee at a local café
Local price: 8–15 MAD Tourist café price: 20–35 MAD
Local cafés in Morocco are extraordinary social institutions — men sitting for hours over a single coffee, watching the street, talking slowly. They are also very cheap. Find them one or two streets away from any main tourist zone and the price drops immediately.
Fresh orange juice
Local price: 10–15 MAD Tourist price: 20–35 MAD
Morocco produces exceptional oranges and fresh juice is everywhere. The famous juice stalls at Jemaa el-Fna in Marrakech charge tourist prices. The identical drink one street away costs half as much.
Water (0.5L bottle)
Local price: 3–5 MAD Tourist price: 8–15 MAD
Buy water at supermarkets and local shops. Never buy it from a cart or stall in a tourist zone unless you enjoy paying three times the real price.

Transport: The Biggest Gap
Transport is where tourists get overcharged most consistently and most aggressively. The price difference between knowing and not knowing can be enormous — especially at airports.
Petit taxi (city taxi) — short ride
Local price: 15–30 MAD Tourist price: 50–80 MAD
Petit taxis in most Moroccan cities operate on meters — or should. The meter is almost never actually broken. When a driver tells you the meter is broken, it means he wants to quote you a tourist price instead of running the meter. The correct response is to either insist on the meter or agree on a price before getting in based on knowing what the ride should cost.
The two most important phrases you need: "Dir compteur" — turn on the meter "Chhal l...?" — how much to...?
Ask the price before getting in. Every time. Without exception.
Petit taxi — longer city ride
Local price: 30–50 MAD Tourist price: 80–150 MAD
The longer the ride, the higher the potential overcharge. A tourist who doesn't know the real price for a 20-minute ride across Agadir will accept 200 MAD without question. The real price is 40–50 MAD.
Agadir airport to city
This is the single most overcharged journey in Morocco for tourists.
Real local price: 150–200 MAD Tourist price: 250–350 MAD
Agree on the price before you get in. Do not get into any taxi at Agadir airport without first saying a number and having the driver agree. If they won't agree to 150–200 MAD, find another driver. There are always other drivers.
Grand taxi intercity routes
Grand taxis are shared long-distance taxis that fill up with passengers heading the same direction. The price is per seat.
Agadir to Marrakech: 100–150 MAD per seat Marrakech to Essaouira: 80–120 MAD per seat
These are fixed shared prices that locals pay. If a driver tries to charge you significantly more, they're either asking you to fill the whole taxi yourself (legitimate but more expensive) or overcharging you. Know the difference.
CTM bus
Morocco's reliable intercity bus network runs on fixed prices that don't change based on who's asking.
Marrakech to Agadir: 100–150 MAD Marrakech to Merzouga (Sahara): 200–250 MAD via Supratours
These are the real prices. Buy your ticket at the CTM or Supratours counter and pay the printed price.

Accommodation: What You Actually Pay
Marrakech
The medina riad market has an enormous price range — from genuine value to genuine robbery — and the price correlates only loosely with quality.
Hostel dorm bed: 150–300 MAD per night Budget to mid-range riad: 400–800 MAD per night Luxury riad: 2,000–5,000 MAD per night
The most important thing to know: a riad priced at 600 MAD is not automatically better than one at 400 MAD. Read actual written reviews, not just star ratings. Check the location inside the medina — some beautiful riads are in alleys that are genuinely difficult to navigate at night.
Agadir
Agadir operates more like a conventional beach resort city — hotels dominate over riads.
Budget hotel: 200–400 MAD per night Mid-range hotel: 500–1,000 MAD per night
Desert camps (Merzouga/Sahara)
Budget camp: 400–700 MAD per night Luxury camp: 1,200–2,500 MAD per night
The difference between budget and luxury here is real — en-suite bathrooms, proper beds, and heating vs shared facilities and basic mattresses. The stars above both are identical.

Shopping: What to Pay and When to Walk Away
Amlou — the Souss region's almond and argan paste
Authentic cooperative price: 80–200 MAD depending on quantity Tourist shop price: 300–500 MAD for an inferior product
The real version is bought from cooperatives or trusted stalls inside Souk El Had in Agadir. The tourist shop version is often diluted with cheap oils. The difference is immediately obvious when you taste them side by side.
Culinary argan oil (250ml, genuine)
Real price: 150–300 MAD Tourist cooperative price: 400–600 MAD
Morocco is full of "argan cooperatives" that are not cooperatives. They are shops with women sitting outside performing traditional processing for tourists while the products inside are commercially produced and massively overpriced. The real cooperatives exist but you need to find them through local recommendation rather than through a guide who takes commission.
Hammam
Local neighbourhood hammam entry: 15–30 MAD Tourist hammam with scrub: 80–150 MAD Luxury tourist hammam: 300–600 MAD
The local neighbourhood hammam is one of the most authentic experiences available in Morocco and costs almost nothing. You need to know the basic etiquette (covered in any decent culture guide) but the experience is completely accessible to tourists willing to step outside the curated version.

The One Rule That Covers Everything
Every price in Morocco has a local version and a tourist version. The gap between them is not a mistake or an injustice — it's simply information asymmetry. Tourists pay more because they don't know what things cost.
You now know what things cost.
The practical application is simple: before any transaction, ask yourself whether you're in the right type of place paying the right type of price. If you're sitting in a restaurant facing a famous square with a menu in four languages, you're paying tourist prices. If you're at a counter with plastic chairs and a handwritten menu and every other customer is Moroccan, you're paying local prices.
Choose accordingly.

A Final Note on Bargaining
Knowing real prices is not the same as being aggressive or disrespectful. Morocco has a bargaining culture that is social and good-natured — it's a negotiation between two people who both know the game.
The goal is never to humiliate a seller or pay zero. The goal is to pay a fair price that reflects what locals actually pay. When you know that price going in, the negotiation is calm, quick, and usually ends with both parties satisfied.
Walking away is always an option. It's also usually the most effective negotiating tool available. A calm "safi, khalih" (okay, leave it) while starting to walk away does more than any amount of argument.
